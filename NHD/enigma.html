              The Enigma

              In 1938, Turing began to work on cracking German military codes at a facility called Bletchley Park.

              One of these ciphers was the Enigma. Looking like a typewriter, the Enigma machine was made of mechanical rotors that produced coded messages.

              An Enigma machine worked by reassigning letters with other letters through a series of moving rotors. Only by knowing the original setting of the rotors could an Enigma message be decoded.
              "An Enigma machine." - Science & Society Picture Library via Getty Images, 1996

              Cracking the Enigma

              Turing discovered a mechanical way to decipher the Enigma. He applied the principles of the Turing Machine to code breaking and improved the Bombe, an earlier attempt at cracking the Enigma. With Turing's contributions, Enigma messages were successfully decoded.

              Turing's work on the Enigma exemplified the practical uses for his Turing Machine, showing that his concepts had a place in everyday life. Using algorithmic thinking, he forever revolutionized computing. 
              "Alan Turing... helped change the course of World War II."
              - (Viehoff)
              “Early on in his stay at Bletchley, it had become evident to Turing that the only way to break a cipher created by a machine would be with a machine.”
              - (Leavitt)
