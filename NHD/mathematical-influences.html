              The Entscheidungsproblem

              "The possibility of having a calculus of reason that one could engage to solve all disagreements quickly, surely, automatically, and accurately."
              - (Henderson)
              The Entscheidungsproblem (German for “Decision Problem”) was a challenge posed by David Hilbert in 1928. The problem asks if a finite process exists to determine whether a mathematical input is "true" or "false."

              For example, the equation 2+3=5 would output "true," but the equation 2+3=7 would output "false."
              "David Hilbert." - University of Göttingen, 1912

              The Incompleteness Theorems

              "Kurt Gödel." - Alfred Eisenstaedt, 1962

              "Any effectively general theory capable of expressing elementary arithmetic cannot be both consistent and complete. In particular, for any consistent, effectively generate formal theory that proves certain basic arithmetic truths, there is an arithmetical statement that is true, but not provable in the theory."
              - Kurt Gödel, trans. Kleene
              Kurt Gödel's First Incompleteness Theory of 1931 states that all arithmetic processes (systems) can be either consistent or complete, but not both; claims made by a system cannot be proved. Turing used this theory to understand that no machine can fully replicate other systems.
              "For any formal effectively generated theory T including basic arithmetical truths and also certain truths about formal provability, if T includes a statement of its own consistency then T is inconsistent."
              - Kurt Gödel, trans. Kleene
              Kurt Gödel's Second Incompleteness Theory was an extension of the First Incompleteness Theory, stating that a system cannot analyze itself.

              The Influence on Turing

              Turing was intrigued by the Entscheidungsproblem and immediately sought a solution. His creativity and motivation inspired the idea of combining mathematics and machines. Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems introduced Turing to the restrictions of these mathematical machines.

              Turing created a machine that allowed him to implement a new form of computation, eventually disproving the Entscheidungsproblem. This machine was the Turing Machine.
              "Alan Turing, British mathematician." - Bill Sanderson
