              Revolution

              "If one were to ask a mathematician of the early 1930s what computing machines might have to do with mathematics, the response would likely be puzzlement. Adding machines or desktop tabulators might be able to do arithmetic, but that would be of little interest to the pure mathematician."
              - (Henderson) 
              "Turing in his late teens or early twenties." -Story Center Productions, 2011
              Turing expanded the use of computation into everyday life. Now, instead of being limited to simple calculations, computation could be used to do anything.
              "The fact remains that everyone who taps at a keyboard, opening a spreadsheet or a word-processing program, is working on an incarnation of a Turing Machine."
              - Paul Gray, Time Magazine
              "Cartoon of a Turing Machine." -University of Pennsylvania, 2010


              His Turing Machine demonstrated that everything could be translated into simple commands. Now, humans could solve any problem without having to understand the math behind it.
              “The Universal Turing Machine was far ahead of its time, as it would be many years before it could be engineered into reality. However, his idea for a machine that could be programmed to perform different tasks essentially describes every digital computer in existence today.”
              - (Beavan)
              The six simple functions that defined the Turing Machine would allow future computers to carry out any operation.               “Turing’s research made a huge contribution towards deciphering German radio messages encrypted using the Enigma machine during World War II... His theoretical work, which still forms the basis of information technology to this day, is equally significant. While his contemporaries could not see beyond the pure calculating capabilities of the computer, Turing designed the model of a universal machine capable of solving every algorithmic problem.”
              - (Ryska)
              Turing's work on the Enigma later introduced practical uses for the Turing Machine. Soon, the first computers (Pilot ACE, ENIAC, and UNIVAC) were created based on Turing's ideas. His machine was no longer theoretical, but was now implemented in the real world.
              "Enigma machine." - Heinz Nixdorf Museums Forum, 2011
              "Visualization of millions of Facebook friendships between cities around the world." - Paul Butler, 2010

              "How well does the model actually fit the real world? ...Turing’s model fits computer programs perfectly."
              - (Main)
              “Turing was a genius and a visionary who foresaw the digital world in which we now live and who believed machines would one day think.”
              - (Harrington)
              Today, computing is the heart of society, connecting everybody digitally. Almost everything relies on computers performing calculations that do not need to be understood by the user. This change began with a man who combined math with machines. He transformed the use of mathematics and applied it to problem solving in everyday life. This was the story of how Alan Turing, an unorthodox mathematician, developed a conceptual computer, the Turing Machine, which revolutionized computation.
              "Our day-to-day lives would be different in how [we] learn things."
              - (Main)
